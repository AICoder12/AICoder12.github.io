Knowledge-sharing prompt network for surface defect segmentation with continual learning

Tiyu Fang1, Lin Zhang1, Jian Wang1, Zhongsheng Xu2, Xiaolei Li1*, Ran Song1
1 School of Control Science and Engineering, Shandong University, Jinan, China
2 The Chinese University of Hong Kong, Shenzhen, Guangdong, China
Email: qylxl@sdu.edu.cn; Group homepage: www.vsislab.com

Abstract

In industrial manufacturing, surface defect segmentation (SDS) plays a vital role in product quality inspection, with deep learning methods becoming the mainstream solution. In practical production, defect data accumulates over time, and new defect classes can emerge at any time. However, when adapting to new classes, existing models tend to suffer from catastrophic forgetting of old classes in the absence of prior data, causing significant accuracy degradation. This work tackles the challenge and introduces continual learning into SDS to investigate how to continuously learn new defect classes without forgetting previously acquired class knowledge. To address this issue, we propose a Knowledge-Sharing Prompt Network (KSPNet), which integrates multi-task learning mechanism with the frozen network to enable retention and continual expansion of knowledge from incremental class training, and fine-tunes complementary prompt embeddings for efficient adaptation to new classes. Specifically, to enable generalized prompt inference on the frozen network, a Knowledge-Balanced Low-Rank Adaptation (KB-LoRA) module is designed to incorporate multi-task low-rank matrices into image encoder, effectively balancing feature learning between new and old defect classes under multi-task loss constraints. Meanwhile, a Complementary Prompt Block (CPBlock) is designed to introduce task-invariant and task-specific prompts to achieve effective knowledge transfer and sharing across incremental classes for guiding accurate defect segmentation. Extensive experiments on the constructed CID-Seg dataset and real-world pipeline welding defect data (WD-Seg dataset) demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms existing approaches and highlights its engineering potential for dynamic industrial SDS.
